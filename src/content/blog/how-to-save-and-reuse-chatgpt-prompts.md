# How to Save and Reuse Your ChatGPT Prompts (Stop Retyping)

**Time to read: 3 minutes**

If you use ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini every day, you've probably rewritten the same prompt a hundred times — your "summarize this in bullet points" prompt, your code-review prompt, your email-rewrite prompt. Retyping them is slow, and the good versions get lost in your chat history.

This guide shows you two ways to save and reuse your prompts: a manual system, and a faster one-click method.

---

## Why You Should Save Your Prompts

A good prompt is a reusable asset. The prompt that gets ChatGPT to produce exactly the output you want took effort to dial in — and every time you rewrite it from memory, you risk getting a worse result. Saving prompts means:

- **Consistency**: the same prompt produces the same quality every time
- **Speed**: no retyping a 6-line prompt from scratch
- **Sharing**: your team can reuse the prompts that actually work
- **Iteration**: you can improve a saved prompt instead of starting over

---

## Method 1: The Manual Way (Notes App)

The zero-tool approach:

1. Keep a notes doc (Notion, Apple Notes, a text file) titled "Prompts"
2. Paste each prompt you want to keep, with a short label
3. When you need one, open the doc, find it, copy it, switch tabs, and paste

This works, but it has friction: you're constantly switching between your notes and the AI tab, and there's no way to insert a prompt directly into the chat box. For a handful of prompts it's fine. Past that, it gets tedious.

## Tip: Turn Repeated Prompts into Templates with Variables

If you often send the same prompt with one thing changed — a language, a tone, a target audience — use a **variable** instead of editing the text each time. For example:

```
Rewrite the text below in a {{tone}} tone for a {{audience}} audience.
```

Save it once, and fill in `tone` and `audience` when you insert it. One template covers dozens of variations.
